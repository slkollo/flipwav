WAV vs MP3 — What's the Difference?

Audio Formats Beginners Guide April 2026 · 5 min read

WAV and MP3 are the two most common audio formats — but they work very differently. One is a huge, perfect copy of the original sound. The other is a clever compression that throws away parts you (probably) can't hear. Here's everything you need to know.

The Simple Answer

WAV = Lossless. Every single bit of audio data is stored. Perfect quality, big file size.

MP3 = Lossy. Audio is compressed by removing frequencies the human ear is less sensitive to. Smaller file, slightly reduced quality.

Think of it like a photograph. WAV is the original RAW file — every pixel preserved. MP3 is a compressed JPEG — smaller but you might notice artifacts if you look close enough.

When to Use WAV

Use WAV

  • Recording music in a studio
  • Editing audio in a DAW
  • Archiving original recordings
  • Professional broadcast/film
  • Sound design for games
  • Mastering tracks

Use MP3

  • Uploading to Spotify / YouTube
  • Sharing via WhatsApp, email
  • Podcast distribution
  • Background music for videos
  • Mobile playback / low storage
  • General everyday listening

Does Quality Actually Matter?

For most people listening casually — no. At 192kbps or higher, MP3 is virtually indistinguishable from WAV to the average ear. Double-blind studies consistently show most people cannot reliably tell the difference.

However, for music production, the difference matters hugely. Every time you edit and re-save an MP3, it compresses again — degrading quality. WAV avoids this completely.

Pro tip: Always record and edit in WAV. Convert to MP3 only at the very end — for the final export you share or upload.
